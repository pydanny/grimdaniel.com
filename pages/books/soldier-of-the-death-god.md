Nekra woke me with a kiss on the cheek. We've been married for over twenty years, but the love is still there.  She's still beautiful, everything that I can ask for in a life partner. She still serves the Lich Queen, once as a handmaid but now as her head seamstress. 

I rolled out of bed, grateful that even in my forties, I have the mobility of youth. My joints do creak, though. Unlike many other dark captains, I am still alive, the heart still beating in my chest. Death is not the end of service to the Lich Queen, but I like the act of breathing. Eventually, the aches and pains of age will slow me down, and I'll cross over. But that day hasn't come yet.

I look in the mirror. I can see a gaunt man with long, gray hair. Handsome of face, only one scar, that on my cheek, received in a fight before I took service to the Lich Queen. My wife gets up and hugs me from behind. She, too, is gaunt, but feminine. She remains the great beauty that made the Dark Mistress select her to be a handmaid.

"Can you walk our children to the academy?" My wife asks. "The streets are not what they were."

I frown, the mirror betrays me as crows' feet appear by my eyes. Recently, the ruffian gangs have been causing trouble again on the streets. Once more, I will need to cull the criminal class so they remember the Lich Queen's mandate: In Necropolis, a child must be able to walk safely from the northern gate to the southern gate with a bag of gold in each hand.

Part of it is that the ruffians know the Dark Mistress is away, invading the primitive Kingdom of Namayan. While she converts that petty, feudal kingdom to righteous worship of the Elemental God of Death, they have come out like the rats they are. No matter, it is well within my ability to bring them to heal.

Nekra lets me go, and I make my way to my private chambers. As I drink a glass of wine, the servants dress me. First in spidersilk undergarments, followed by a jacket of arctic wool. Over that goes my armor, perfectly fitting bone-steel plate. Bone-steel is forged from iron and human bone, making a pale yet light alloy. As a Dark Captain of the Death Legions, my armor is painted in royal black. The exception is where protective runes have been scribed, the power of which burns away the paint.

Once garbed in my armor of state, I personally buckle my belt on, for the scabbarded soulblade it holds is a danger to anyone but me. Then Nekra comes in, also now dressed, carrying my blood drinking rune spear. Together, we leave our chambers and out into the courtyard of our home.

Our home is a five-story house built around a central courtyard. Our chambers are fashionably on the bottom floor, as are those of our children, and the stables. The family servants and guards live in the upper floors; their lower status means they climb the steep stairs to their residences and workplaces. It's the property the Lich Queen gave me as a wedding present twenty years ago.

Our children come out of the apartment they share, dressed for the Ashen Spire Academy of Sovereign Arts in their school uniform of black leathers, replete with a weapons belt containing a sword, and custom ritual daggers. Morcant, our son, nineteen, is tall and his handsome visage clearly comes from Nekra. Morrigan, our daughter, and a year younger, looks like a mix of Nekra and me. Her soft face hides a stubborn rage that reminds me of my youth.

Nekra bids them farewell for the day from afar. Here in the courtyard, we must keep up appearances. Our children's servants have filed out behind them and bow as we exit the gates. I can read the pride that goes in those bows, a pride for which the servants have every right to feel. While the blood of my ancestors flows in the veins of Morcant and Morrigan, the servants did most of the raising. Both me and Nekra had important roles to fulfill, and as our noble class does, we passed the task of raising and caring for children to these folk. I am grateful to these servants and protect them as one does any precious resource.

Outside the gates of our home, a dead woman sweeps leaves while a dead man pulls a cart laden with packages. To someone not new to Necropolis, their smell would be oppressive. To me, the smell is home, the place where many commoners are lifted above simple labor through the expediency of the undead workforce. Instead, the people have lives of leisure, spending their time pushing the boundaries of knowledge and art. Only in backwards places like Namayan are the peasants forced to spend their living years in labor, and then have wasted deaths spent entirely outside our plane of existence. We are more enlightened than they are, for which we are thankful to her Dark Majesty, the Lich Queen, head priestess of the God of Death.

I lead the way, walking at a stately pace, my children walking beside me. I'm escorting my brood, and want to see why Nekra thinks the streets are getting uneasy.

We walk to my children's school through the narrow streets of Necropolis, passing shops and the homes above them, as well as the inevitable temples and columbaria, where the remains of the undead worked to dust, and ashes are kept. As we approach the ashen spire that is second in height to only the Lich Queen's massive bone tower in the center of the city, we pass through the grand square of the city. Merchants hawk their wares from a hundred stalls, buskers try to convince the hard-working people of the city to part with their coin, artists work on easels, and the walking dead keep everything clean.

I don't need to see their faces to know my children are rolling their eyes as I veer to approach the stall of Mildred Graves. I live a mostly ascetic life, living plainly, partaking only in that which a married man who serves our ruler as Dark Captain should. Here, in the square, Mrs. Graves is one of the few liberties I allow myself. 

I have eaten at the best restaurants in Necropolis and the tables of visiting emperors paying tribute to the Lich Queen. Yet nothing compares to the food that Mrs. Graves prepares at her stall. Bits of chicken liver are pan-seared until perfectly crispy, over which a cooked lizard egg is place, then the result is wrapped in a crowmeal crepe. I pay her handsomely, a silver coin, which she weighs. I could take murderous offense to the fact that she doesn't trust me to proper coin, but her cooking artistry is such that I grant her allowances.
