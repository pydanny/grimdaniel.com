## Blurb

_Cryptus is a Dark Captain in service to the Lich Queen. A loyal vassal and righteous worshipper of the Elemental God of Death, he has been amply rewarded for his service with title, property, and happy family.  His rigid moral code defines him as much as the dreaded necromancy he wields._

_Can Cryptus' rigid moral code survive when the Lich Queen is defeated and everything he knows falls apart?_

_This is a story of violence, betrayal, and despair._

## Chapter 1: Cryptus

Nekra woke me with a kiss on the cheek. We've been married for over twenty years, but the love is still there.  She's still beautiful, everything that I can ask for in a life partner. She still serves the Lich Queen, once as a handmaid but now as her head seamstress. 



I rolled out of bed, grateful that even in my forties, I have the mobility of youth. My joints do creak, though. Unlike many other dark captains, I am still alive, the heart still beating in my chest. Death is not the end of service to the Lich Queen, but I like the act of breathing. Eventually, the aches and pains of age will slow me down, and I'll cross over. But that day hasn't come yet.



I look in the mirror. I can see a gaunt man with long, gray hair. Handsome of face, only one scar, that on my cheek, received in a fight before I took service to the Lich Queen. My wife gets up and hugs me from behind. She, too, is gaunt, but feminine. She remains the great beauty that made the Dark Mistress select her to be a handmaid.



"Can you walk our children to the academy?" My wife asks. "The streets are not what they were."



I frown, the mirror betrays me as crows' feet appear by my eyes. Recently, the ruffian gangs have been causing trouble again on the streets. Once more, I will need to cull the criminal class so they remember the Lich Queen's mandate: In Necropolis, a child must be able to walk safely from the northern gate to the southern gate with a bag of gold in each hand.



Part of it is that the ruffians know the Dark Mistress is away, invading the primitive Kingdom of Namayan. While she converts that petty, feudal kingdom to righteous worship of the Elemental God of Death, they have begun to push boundaries. No matter, it is well within my ability to bring them to heal.



Nekra lets me go, and I make my way to my private chambers. As I drink a glass of wine, the servants dress me. First in spidersilk undergarments, followed by a jacket of arctic wool. Over that goes my armor, perfectly fitting bone-steel plate. Bone-steel is forged from iron and human bone, making a pale yet light alloy. As a Dark Captain of the Death Legions, my armor is painted in royal black. The exception is where protective runes have been scribed, the power of which burns away the paint.



Once garbed in my armor of state, I personally buckle my belt on, for the scabbarded soulblade it holds is a danger to anyone but me. Then Nekra comes in, also now dressed, carrying my blood drinking rune spear. Together, we leave our chambers and out into the courtyard of our home.



Our home is a five-story house built around a central courtyard. Our chambers are fashionably on the bottom floor, as are those of our children, and the stables. The family servants and guards live in the upper floors; their lower status means they climb the steep stairs to their residences and workplaces. It's the property the Lich Queen gave me as a wedding present twenty years ago.



Our children come out of the apartment they share, dressed for the Ashen Spire Academy of Sovereign Arts in their school uniform of black leather, replete with a weapons belt containing a sword, and custom ritual daggers. Morcant, our son, seventeen, is tall, and his handsome visage clearly comes from Nekra. Morrigan, our daughter, who is a year younger, looks like a mix of Nekra and me. Her soft face hides a stubborn rage that reminds me of my youth.



Nekra bids them farewell for the day from afar. Here in the courtyard, we must keep up appearances. Our children's servants have filed out behind them and bow as we exit the gates. I can read the pride that goes in those bows, a pride for which the servants have every right to feel. While the blood of my ancestors flows in the veins of Morcant and Morrigan, the servants did most of the raising. Both Nekra and I had important roles to fulfill, and as our noble class does, we passed the task of raising and caring for children to these folk. I am grateful to these servants and protect them as one would any precious resource.



Outside the gates of our home, a dead woman sweeps leaves while a dead man pulls a cart laden with packages. To someone not new to Necropolis, their smell would be oppressive. To me, the smell is home, the place where many commoners are lifted above simple labor through the expediency of the undead workforce. Instead, the people have lives of leisure, spending their time pushing the boundaries of knowledge and art. Only in backwards places like Namayan are the peasants forced to spend their living years in labor, and then have wasted deaths spent entirely outside our plane of existence. We are more enlightened than they are, for which we are thankful to her Dark Majesty, the Lich Queen, head priestess of the God of Death.



I lead the way, walking at a stately pace, my children walking beside me. I'm escorting my brood, and want to see why Nekra thinks the streets are getting uneasy.



We walk to my children's school through the narrow streets of Necropolis, passing shops and the homes above them, as well as the inevitable temples and columbaria, where the remains of the undead worked to dust, and ashes are kept. As we approach the ashen spire that is second in height to only the Lich Queen's massive bone tower in the center of the city, we pass through the grand square of the city. Merchants hawk their wares from a hundred stalls, buskers try to convince the hard-working people of the city to part with their coin, artists work on easels, and the walking dead keep everything clean.



I don't need to see their faces to know my children are rolling their eyes as I veer to approach the stall of Mildred Graves. I live a mostly ascetic life, living plainly, partaking only in that which a married man who serves our ruler as Dark Captain should. Here, in the square, Mrs. Graves is one of the few liberties I allow myself. 



I have eaten at the best restaurants in Necropolis and the tables of visiting emperors paying tribute to the Lich Queen. Yet nothing compares to the food that Mrs. Graves prepares at her stall. Bits of chicken liver are pan-seared until perfectly crispy, over which a cooked lizard egg is placed, then the result is wrapped in a crowmeal crepe. I pay her handsomely, a silver coin, which she weighs. I could take murderous offense to the fact that she doesn't trust me to proper coin, but her cooking artistry is such that I grant her allowances.



As Mrs. Graves prepares breakfast for me, Morcant, and Morrigan, I observe the crowd. Ruffians walk in small groups amongst them, the common people wary of the wolves within their midst. Every ruffian wore the signature collar: a broad, soot-gray ruff whose stiffened folds framed the face like the petals of a poisoned flower. Once the badge of courtiers and merchants, it had become, in the tight streets of Necropolis, a mark of cutthroat self-appointed protectors. The lace was never clean. Most collars were patched with burlap, a few embroidered with silver thread.



The street gangs are allowed to exist because they serve a purpose. The Lich Queen's early experiment with a constabulary saw corruption hiding behind her uniform, so she replaced them with criminals who were more obvious in operation. So she replaced them with the already present street gangs, who are supposed to be the sort of mix of vigilante and criminal oppression that happens in every city. They also manage that unsavory element of human nature: cheap physical thrills.



In any case, my wife was right: the ruffians were pushing boundaries even here in the grand market square under the shadow of the Lich Queen's tallest tower. Competing gangs worked the merchants, forcing the hard-working vendors to pay different groups to avoid trouble. That was against the unspoken rule where gangs could not overlap, which meant too many young men were not joining the legions. I would need to reduce the gangs again.

I stifled an internal sigh. Before I reduced the gangs again I would need to talk to one of their leaders, Balchor. We had attended the Ashen Spire together, and he had competed with me for the affections of Nekra. While I had completed my education there, he had left in anger.



Mrs. Graves snapped her fingers impatiently at me. I took the freshly cooked wrapped meat and egg from her. I walked my children to the edge of the square, where the gate into Ashen Spire stood. 

My children walked in without even a farewell. Like the ruffians, my children are pushing boundaries. I suppose that is what teenagers do, even if their father is a Dark Captain serving the greatest ruler in history. I shrug, they will come around in a few years.



_ASSIST ME!_



The psychic voice of the Lich Queen slams into my head with enough force to drive me to my knees. The power of the sending is such that I know every Dark Captain has received it. And through the connection, I feel an emotion I never imagined she could feel.



Fear. 



Her Dark Majesty is afraid.
